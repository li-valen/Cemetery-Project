  1. Birth and Early Education

    February 8, 1820

    Born in Williamsport, PA, to Francis C. and Jane H. Campbell. Grew up in an affluent family; his father was a prominent lawyer.


    Historical Context

    Campbell was born the same year as the Missouri Compromise, which temporarily resolved slavery disputes. This would later shape his anti-slavery stance in Congress.

  2. Legal Education and Career Beginnings

    1840-1841

    Graduated from Dickinson Law College and admitted to the bar. Moved to Pottsville, PA, establishing a successful law practice and mentoring future lawyers.


    Philadelphia Connection

    During the 1840s-1870s, Philadelphia became a hub for manufacturing and railroads. Campbell's law firm at 130 S. 6th Street (Lawyer's Row) thrived during this industrial boom.

  3. Marriage and Family

    June 1843

    Married Juliet Hamersley Lewis, daughter of Pennsylvania Chief Justice Ellis Lewis. The couple had four children, two of whom died in the Civil War.

  4. Congressional Career

    1855-1857, 1859-1863

    Elected to the 34th, 36th, and 37th Congresses as a Whig/Republican. Advocated against slavery, supported tariffs, and sponsored the Pacific Railroad Act (1862).


    National Context

    Campbell's campaign against the Kansas-Nebraska Act in 1854 occurred when Philadelphia's industries were expanding, reflecting national tensions over slavery following the Mexican-American War (1846-1848).

  5. Civil War Service

    1861-1863

    Enlisted as a private in Cassius Clay's Washington Guards; later became Major of the 25th PA Volunteers. Helped repel Lee's invasion of Pennsylvania in 1863.


    Philadelphia Connection

    As Campbell helped pursue Lee's army, Philadelphia factories supplied Union troops with munitions and uniforms, linking his personal service to the city's wartime role. His sons fought in the war, and his daughter Julia returned to Philadelphia after her husband's death.

  6. Diplomatic Service in Sweden

    1864-1867

    Appointed Minister Resident to Sweden by Lincoln. Strengthened U.S.-Sweden relations and thwarted Confederate plots in Europe.

  7. Later Life

    1867-1895

    Retired to Philadelphia and later the "Aeola" estate in Wayne, PA. Continued legal practice throughout this period.


    National Achievement

    In 1869, the Transcontinental Railroad was completed, connecting the Central Pacific and Union Pacific railroads. Campbell's Pacific Railroad Act (1862) had laid the groundwork for this national achievement during his congressional service.

  8. Centennial Exposition

    1876

    Philadelphia hosted the World's Fair celebrating U.S. progress. Campbell's legal work coincided with the city's national prominence during this period.

  9. Death

    1893-1895

    Campbell died of heart failure in 1895. His passing followed the Panic of 1893, an economic depression that marked the end of the Gilded Age.


    Final Connection

    Campbell's death in 1895 following the Panic of 1893 symbolized the close of an era he helped shape through legislation and diplomacy.
