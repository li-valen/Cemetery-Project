  1. Birth of Samuel Hepburn

    1698

    James H. Campbell’s great-grandfather, Samuel Hepburn, was born in Glasgow, Scotland. His descendants later settled in America to escape religious persecution.

  2. Marriage of Samuel Hepburn and Janet Sinclair

    1746

    Samuel Hepburn married Janet Sinclair in Scotland before the family relocated to Ireland and eventually to America.

  3. Hepburn Family Emigrates to America

    1773

    James Hepburn, Campbell’s grandfather, and his brother William sailed from Ireland to Philadelphia in search of new opportunities.

  4. Marriage of James Hepburn and Mary Hopewell

    1781

    Campbell’s grandparents wed, uniting families that would eventually shape a lasting legacy in America.

  5. Birth of Francis C. Campbell

    1787

    Francis C. Campbell, who would become a prominent lawyer, was born in York, Pennsylvania.

  6. Era of Good Feelings Begins

    1816

    The period of national unity under President James Monroe began, coinciding with important family milestones for the Campbells.

  7. First Public School in Philadelphia

    1818

    Philadelphia established its first public school, marking a significant step in educational reform.

  8. Birth and the Missouri Compromise

    February 8, 1820

    Born in Williamsport, Pennsylvania, to Francis C. Campbell (a prominent lawyer) and Jane Hepburn Campbell.

    Historical Context: Campbell’s birth coincided with the Missouri Compromise, which temporarily resolved tensions over slavery by admitting Missouri as a slave state and Maine as a free state. This event shaped his later political career, as he became a staunch opponent of slavery’s expansion.

  9. Birth and Early Education

    February 8, 1820

    Born in Williamsport, PA, to Francis C. and Jane H. Campbell. Grew up in an affluent family; his father was a prominent lawyer.


  10. Infrastructure and Transportation Advances

    1824

    The Pennsylvania Railroad Company was formed and the Erie Canal opened, revolutionizing trade and transportation in the Northeast. Also, Courtland Howell’s father passed away, impacting his early life.

  11. Legal Education and Career Beginnings

    1840-1841

    Graduated from Dickinson Law College in August 1841 and was admitted to the bar on October 18, 1841. Moved to Pottsville, PA, where he established a successful law practice and mentored future lawyers.


  12. Marriage and Family

    June 1843

    Married Juliet Hamersley Lewis, daughter of Pennsylvania Chief Justice Ellis Lewis. The couple had four children, two of whom died in the Civil War.


  13. Political Awakening

    1844

    Campbell attended the Whig National Convention supporting Henry Clay, amid anti‑immigration riots targeting Irish Catholic communities.

  14. Mexican-American War

    1846–1848

    The United States expanded its territory during the Mexican‑American War, acquiring lands in the Southwest.

  15. Seneca Falls Convention

    1848

    The landmark Seneca Falls Convention ignited the women’s rights movement in America.

  16. Gold Rush and Cholera Outbreak

    1849

    The California Gold Rush spurred mass migration to the West, while a cholera outbreak in Philadelphia highlighted serious public health challenges.

  17. Fugitive Slave Act Passed

    1850

    The controversial Fugitive Slave Act intensified national tensions over slavery.

  18. Compromise of 1850 Passed

    1850

    The controversial package including the Fugitive Slave Act became law, intensifying national tensions over slavery - shaping Campbell's later anti-slavery stance.

  19. Hosting Local Celebrations in Pottsville

    1851

    Campbell hosted local celebrations, parades, and community events in Pottsville, reflecting his influence in both legal and social spheres.

  20. Election to 34th Congress

    1855-1857

    Won election as Opposition Whig representing PA-11. Served on Ways & Means Committee, blocking attempts to lower tariffs.

  21. Congressional Career

    1855–1857, 1859–1863

    Elected to the 34th, 36th, and 37th Congresses as a Whig/Republican, Campbell championed anti‑slavery measures, supported protective tariffs, and sponsored the Pacific Railroad Act (1862). He notably failed to secure election to the 35th Congress.


    National Context

    His campaign against the Kansas‑Nebraska Act in 1854 took place during a period of rapid industrial expansion in Philadelphia following the Mexican‑American War.


  22. Opposition to Kansas-Nebraska Act

    March 10, 1856

    Delivered fiery House speech condemning "Bleeding Kansas" violence and the repeal of Missouri Compromise restrictions.

  23. Speech on the Kansas–Nebraska Act

    1856

    In a passionate address, Campbell condemned the Kansas–Nebraska Act for undermining the Missouri Compromise and expanding slavery.

  24. Failed Bid for the 35th Congress

    1857

    Despite his growing influence, Campbell narrowly lost his bid for a seat in the 35th Congress, a setback that would shape his future political strategies.

  25. Re-election to 36th Congress

    1859-1861

    Defeated incumbent William Dewart, serving on Elections Committee and opposing Southern secession attempts.

  26. Committee of Thirty-Three Appointment

    December 1860

    As PA's representative, opposed Crittenden Compromise and Corwin Amendment during secession crisis.

  27. Start of Civil War

    1861–1863

    Enlisted as a private in Cassius Clay's Washington Guards within two weeks of the first shots at Sumter, he later became Major of the 25th PA Volunteers and helped repel Lee's invasion of Pennsylvania in 1863.


    Historical Context

    The Civil War began April 12, 1861 with Confederate forces firing on Fort Sumter. Campbell's quick enlistment reflects the immediate Northern response to preserve the Union.


  28. Enlistment in Washington Guards

    April 1861

    Joined Cassius Clay's battalion within days of Fort Sumter - the first Union troops defending DC.

  29. Service in 25th PA Volunteers

    April-July 1861

    Elected Major in 3-month regiment guarding Washington defenses, earning "First Defender" honors.

  30. Interview with President Lincoln

    Early 1862

    While serving during the Civil War, Campbell interviewed President Lincoln and his wife, highlighting his close connection with the administration.

  31. Pacific Railroad Act Passage

    July 1, 1862

    As House committee chairman, sponsored landmark legislation enabling transcontinental railroad construction.

  32. Letters of Defeat and Lincoln's Regret

    December 1862

    After his defeat in the 1862 Congressional elections, Campbell recounted in letters to his wife how Secretary of the Senate John W. Forney notified him of President Lincoln’s particular regret over his political loss, hinting at a close personal connection.

  33. Formation of 39th PA Volunteers

    July 1863

    Raised regiment as Lt. Colonel to pursue Lee after Gettysburg, mustered out after 1 month of service.

  34. Reception at the White House

    January 1863

    In January 1863, Campbell was received by Mrs. Abraham Lincoln at the White House, where he, alongside General Simon Cameron, “gossiped” with the First Lady about her Southern heritage.

  35. Enrollment Act Vote

    February 25, 1863

    On February 25, 1867, Campbell voted in favor of the monumental Enrollment Act (Civil War Military Draft Act), demonstrating his vigorous military methods and intolerance for secession.

  36. Appointment as Minister to Sweden

    May 18, 1864

    Lincoln's diplomatic appointment recognizing his congressional service and anti-slavery credentials.

  37. Diplomatic Service in Sweden

    1864–1867

    Appointed Minister Resident to Sweden by Lincoln, Campbell strengthened U.S.–Sweden relations and thwarted Confederate plots in Europe.


  38. Reconstruction Amendments Ratified

    1865-1870

    The transformative Reconstruction Amendments reshaped American democracy:

    • 13th Amendment (1865): Abolished slavery nationwide
    • 14th Amendment (1868): Established birthright citizenship and equal protection
    • 15th Amendment (1870): Prohibited voting discrimination based on race

    Campbell's Legacy

    Having fought against slavery since the 1850s, Campbell witnessed this constitutional transformation while serving as U.S. Minister to Sweden, continuing his advocacy through diplomatic channels.

  39. European Journey and Free Travel Request

    May 23, 1865

    In early 1865, it is likely that Campbell traveled across Europe—specifically France and Paris. On May 23, the Swedish Prime Minister’s Office for External Affairs requested that Prussia, Austria, Switzerland, Italy, and France grant him free travel after he expressed his desire to travel, possibly influenced by the shifting political landscape following the Second Schleswig War.

  40. Diplomatic Correspondence on Lincoln's Assassination

    June 1865

    Following Lincoln's assassination, while in Sweden, Campbell managed key diplomatic communications conveying national grief and solidarity.

  41. Diplomatic Decision by the Johnson Administration

    November 15, 1866

    On November 15, 1866, the Johnson administration decided that John McGinnis would replace Campbell as Minister Resident and that Campbell would be appointed as Minister Resident to Colombia in Bogota. However, these decisions were not passed by the Senate, and McGinnis’ appointment was eventually rebuffed, so Campbell never met him in Paris.

  42. Election to the Military Order of the Loyal Legion (MOLLUS)

    February 1867

    Upon returning to the United States, Campbell was honored by being elected a Companion of the Military Order of the Loyal Legion of the United States, acknowledging his Civil War service.

  43. Farewell with King Charles

    March 29, 1867

    On March 29, 1867, Campbell presented a recall and exchanged thanks with King Charles in the Royal Palace, receiving a ceremonious farewell from the Royal family.

  44. Rejection of Colombia Appointment

    May 1867

    After his term as Minister Resident to Sweden-Norway ended, President Andrew Johnson offered Campbell a second diplomatic post as Minister Resident to Colombia in Bogota. Campbell rejected the offer in protest of the administration’s sympathetic stance toward the South.

  45. Later Life

    1867–1895

    Retired to Philadelphia and later settled at the "Aeola" estate in Wayne, PA, Campbell continued his legal practice and was elected a Companion of the Military Order of the Loyal Legion of the United States.


  46. Return to Philadelphia & Resumption of Legal Practice

    August 1867

    After his diplomatic service, Campbell returned to Philadelphia, establishing his law firm at 130 S. 6th Street and reinforcing his influence in local legal and political spheres.

  47. Completion of the Transcontinental Railroad

    1869

    The completion of the Transcontinental Railroad in 1869 was a national milestone built upon the foundation of the Pacific Railroad Act, which Campbell helped champion.

  48. Economic and Cultural Shifts

    1873

    The Panic of 1873 triggered an economic depression, while cultural milestones like the opening of the Philadelphia Zoo signaled shifting social dynamics.

  49. Centennial Exposition

    1876

    Philadelphia hosted the World's Fair to celebrate U.S. innovation and progress during its centennial.


  50. Rise of Monopolies

    1879

    Philadelphia thrived as a manufacturing hub during the Gilded Age. Campbell’s legal career overlapped with the rise of monopolies like Standard Oil and Carnegie Steel, which dominated industries through aggressive consolidation.

  51. Chinese Exclusion Act

    1882

    Continued legal work in Philadelphia, where Chinese immigrants settled after fleeing racial violence in the West.

    Historical Context: The Chinese Exclusion Act barred Chinese laborers from entering the U.S., reflecting nativist tensions. Campbell likely witnessed Philadelphia’s growing Chinatown and the struggles of immigrant communities.

  52. Legal Landmarks

    1883

    Campbell argued the landmark Packer Estate Case before the Pennsylvania Supreme Court, and the establishment of standard time zones streamlined railroad schedules.

  53. 50th Wedding Anniversary

    1893

    Campbell celebrated his 50th wedding anniversary at the Aeola estate in Wayne, PA, marking half a century of marriage.

  54. Death of James Hepburn Campbell

    1895

    Campbell died of heart failure in 1895, marking the end of an influential era in law and politics—his passing followed the economic downturn of the Panic of 1893.
